# The Alabama photo sorter's field guide to $8-plus vintage prints

Out of any random box of 20th-century photographs, roughly **80–90 percent will be sub-$5 generics, 10–15 percent will be solid $8–$50 sellers, and 1–5 percent will be $50–$500+ "finds."** Your edge as a reseller is the speed at which you can correctly identify what is what — and almost all of that identification happens on the **back of the photo**, not the front. This guide is organized by decade from the 1940s through the 2000s, with a universal value-drivers section up front and a sorting workflow at the end. The single most important habit to build is flipping every photo over. Stamps, snipes, captions, names, and dates frequently quadruple value, and skipping the back is how dealers leave the most money on the table.

The market has also shifted: **press photo prices peaked in 2021** and softened at the very top end through 2024, but the mid-tier ($50–$500) on eBay remains strong, and hip-hop, women's sports, LGBTQ vernacular, and Black Americana are the fastest-appreciating niches as of 2026. For an Alabama-based seller, geographic provenance is a quiet superpower — Southern small towns are undersupplied relative to the Northeast and California, and Alabama-specific civil rights, NASCAR, Crimson Tide, Muscle Shoals, Tuskegee, and Huntsville/NASA material has dedicated buyers willing to overpay.

## Universal value drivers that push a photo past $8

Twelve factors elevate any photo from "common" to collectible, and the highest prices come when several of them stack on the same print.

**Identification is the single strongest multiplier.** A snapshot of a serviceman is a $3 photo; the same snapshot inscribed "Pvt. James Pritchard, 442nd RCT, Bruyères, France, Oct. 1944" is a $40–$150 photo. Named people, named ships, named aircraft, named towns, dated events — written or visible — change everything. A named Inuit child sells at $400–$450 in auction; the anonymous equivalent sells at $200 or less. **Crossover collector appeal stacks value the same way**: a single image of Black railroad workers at a named Alabama depot touches railroad collectors, postcard topographicals, town historians, African American history institutions, AND occupational collectors. Ask of every photo, "Who else besides the obvious audience would want this?"

**Format hierarchy** matters enormously when subject is equal. Within press photos, the PSA Type 1 / Type 2 / Type 3 / Type 4 system (defined by Yee, Oser, and Fogel in 2005's *A Portrait of Baseball Photography*) is the largest single value multiplier in the entire vintage photo market. A Type 1 — a first-generation print from the original negative made within roughly two years of the photo being taken — can sell for **5 to 50 times** the price of a Type 3 or Type 4 reprint of the exact same image. A Mickey Mantle Type 1 from his 1951 rookie year sold for **$843,750** at Heritage in April 2024. A Cobb Carl Horner T206 source image hit **$396,000**. The same images as Type 4 modern reprints are worth $10–$25.

**Other major drivers** include photographer or studio attribution (a blindstamp by Hurrell, Karsh, Avedon, Jim Marshall, or Bob Gruen can move a photo from $5 to $500+); written information on the back (captions, dates, names, locations); historical significance and news-event connection; identifiable LOCATION, especially small towns and named storefronts; condition (sharp focus, good contrast, no creases or foxing — though subject overrides condition); age as a tiebreaker only; provenance from a known photographer's or journalist's estate; rarity/scarcity of the subject; and signed authenticity with a credible COA from PSA/DNA, JSA, or Beckett. **Subject always trumps age** — a 1970s storefront with prices on the signs beats an anonymous 1880s farmer cabinet card.

## Format identification primer

A reseller needs to be fluent in six format families and the verso markings that date and authenticate them.

**Real photo postcards (RPPCs)** are continuous-tone silver-gelatin prints on postcard stock — not halftone prints. Under a 10× loupe an RPPC shows smooth grayscale and a real photographic emulsion; a printed postcard shows a dot rosette. RPPCs often have "silvering" (metallic sheen) in dark areas when tilted, which authenticates them. The **stamp box** on the back is the dating key:

| Stamp box | Era | Stamp box | Era |
|---|---|---|---|
| AZO (4 triangles up) | 1904–1918 | DEFENDER (diamond inside) | 1920–1940 |
| AZO (2 up, 2 down) | 1918–1930 | DOPS | 1925–1942 |
| AZO (squares in corners) | 1925–1940s | AGFA/ANSCO (2 stars) | 1930s–1960 |
| VELOX (diamonds) | 1907–1914 | EKC | 1940–1950 |
| VELOX (no diamonds) | 1909–1929 | EKO | 1942–1970 |
| CYKO | 1904–1920s | KODAK (alone) | 1950–present |
| NOKO | 1907–1920s | Devolite Peerless | 1950+ |
| ARGO | 1905–1920 | EKKP | 1904–1950 |

Undivided backs are pre-March 1907; divided backs are 1907 onward. A postmark is the *earliest* a card was used — cards can sit years before mailing.

**Press photos** are 7×9 or 8×10 glossy fiber-paper prints distributed by news services to newspapers and magazines. Look on the back for paper "snipes" (typed caption strips glued on), rubber-stamped dates in purple or red ink, agency stamps, photographer credit stamps, newspaper morgue stamps, and grease-pencil crop marks. Major US wire-service date ranges: **ACME 1923–1952, Associated Press from 1926, AP Wirephoto from 1935, International News Service (INS) 1909–1957, NEA 1923–1952, UPI from 1958, UPI Telephoto from 1958, Wide World 1919–1941, Globe Photos 1940s+, AP Laserphoto from late 1970s, Reuters expanded in the US through the 1980s, Getty Images from 1995, WireImage from 2001.** ACME stamps disappear after 1952; INS stamps disappear after 1957; UPI stamps cannot predate 1958; Getty cannot predate 1995. These dates are absolute boundaries.

To distinguish a **Type 1** from later generations, check: period-correct double-weight fiber paper with warm/cream tone (not resin-coated post-1970s paper); date stamp within ~2 years of the image's subject; original snipe captions; editor's grease-pencil crop marks on the front; no "Printed Later" or "File Copy" stamps. **Wire photos** (the *received* end of an AP Wirephoto, UPI Telephoto, or Acme Wirephoto transmission) reveal themselves under a loupe by faint horizontal scan lines, jagged edges along objects, and slightly soft sharpness. Wire photos are Type 3 by definition — collectible but priced well below Type 1 originals.

**NASA "Red Number" originals** require three confirming markers: the mission ID printed in red ink in the margin (e.g., AS11-40-5903); an "A Kodak Paper" watermark on the back; and ideally a purple verso stamp with the NASA logo, Manned Spacecraft Center or Johnson Space Center, and the typed caption. Without all three, treat as a later reprint worth under $10. With all three, even routine Apollo images sell for $300–$2,000+, with iconic frames (Earthrise, the boot print, Aldrin on the ladder, the Blue Marble) reaching $2,000–$15,000+.

**Hollywood and TV publicity 8×10s** are identifiable by studio markings — "Property of MGM," "Kindly Credit Paramount," 20th Century-Fox numeric codes, RKO/Warner Bros./Universal/Columbia/UA back stamps — plus glued snipe captions naming the film, star, and release year. "R57" or "R62" in the lower border indicates a re-release year, NOT original release; reissues are worth less. Period publicity stills are double-weight glossy with knife-cut or deckled edges; modern reprints have visible inkjet dot patterns under a loupe.

**Color slides** are dated by mount: 1941–1949 Kodachrome has red-bordered gray cardboard with red "Kodachrome Transparency" lettering; 1950–1955 has the same red border without month codes; 1955–1961 introduces stamped dates in red ink, then yellow corners with the Kodak yellow-and-red logo (6/1958 onward); from 9/1961 the date is embossed directly into the cardboard (e.g., "JUL 63"). Kodachrome retains color indefinitely; Ektachrome (blue-bordered) shifts magenta with age. **Polaroids** are dated by film type: peel-apart roll film Land Camera 1948–1963, Type 100 peel-apart pack film 1963 onward, integral SX-70 from 1972 (square 3.1" image, thin border), 600 film from 1981 (thicker bottom border, ISO 640), Spectra from 1986 (wider rectangle).

## The 1940s

The 1940s box is defined by **WWII military content** and the apex of the press-photo era. Press photos are this decade's single highest-yield format. Any 8×10 glossy with ACME, AP, UPI, INS, Wide World, Pacific & Atlantic, or Underwood & Underwood stamps and an intact paper snipe carries an **automatic $8–$15 floor**, with named events and named athletes/celebrities jumping to $25–$500+. RPPCs continue at high volume (EKC and AZO-squares are the giveaway 1940s stamp boxes) and routinely fetch $8–$50 for decent subjects, $50–$300+ for gas stations, Southern small-town main streets, identified Black subjects, military scenes, or disasters. Real 1948–49 Polaroids exist but are vanishingly rare; treat any sepia peel-back Polaroid with rough edges as a $20–$100+ candidate.

**WWII is the volume category**. Anonymous GI portraits and basic training shots are $8–$15; **identified soldiers with unit, location, and date** written on the back jump to $15–$50. **Nose art photographs** — any painted pin-up or aircraft name like "Memphis Belle" or "Enola Gay" on a B-17, B-24, or B-29 — are the runaway winner of the decade at $50–$200, more for ID'd crews and named planes. **Pacific Theater combat snapshots, Iwo Jima/Okinawa imagery, paratroopers ID'd to 82nd/101st Airborne, and Tuskegee Airmen photographs** hit $50–$150 routinely. The genuine elite tier is **Holocaust liberation imagery** taken by US soldiers at Buchenwald, Dachau, Ohrdruf, Mauthausen, or Bergen-Belsen — single GI snapshots regularly clear $75–$300, archives with provenance reach four to five figures, and two AP Iwo Jima photos famously sold for $6,000.

The **1940s Hollywood market** runs on Bogart, Bacall, Cary Grant, Hepburn, Bogart, Tracy, Cagney, Stewart, Gable, Rita Hayworth's *Gilda* era, Veronica Lake, and Betty Grable pin-ups. Studio-stamped originals sell at $25–$200; photographer-credited Hurrell, Clarence Sinclair Bull, or Bachrach prints push to $100+. **Sports Type 1s of this decade are blue chip**: Joe DiMaggio, Ted Williams, Stan Musial, Bob Feller, and Hank Greenberg run $100–$500+; a **1947 Jackie Robinson Type 1 routinely brings $5,000–$54,000+**; the 1948 Nat Fein "Babe Bows Out" Type 1 hit $75,000 at Heritage in 2022.

The sleeper tiers are **gas stations with brand signage** (Texaco, Sinclair, Pure Oil, Shell, Standard — a 1930s gas station RPPC sold for $1,136), **Southern small-town main streets** (Alabama RPPCs are undersupplied — $50–$200 with strong content), **dignified Black American family and occupational portraits** ($15–$75, more for Negro League baseball, jazz musicians, or named civil rights pioneers), **affectionate-men servicemen snapshots** for the LGBTQ market ($8–$50), Halloween and eerie costume snapshots ($10–$40), and original press-photo snipe captions that resellers routinely throw away — that paper *is* half the value.

## The 1950s

The 1950s deepens the Hollywood market into its golden age while introducing Korea, early rock and roll, the peak of roadside Americana, and the first civil rights press photos. RPPC production declined as chrome postcards took over after 1939, but RPPCs with a plain KODAK stamp box (1950+), EKO, or AGFA/ANSCO (two stars) continued through the decade and command $10–$100 for strong subjects. Press photos remain dominant; ACME merged into UPI in 1952, INS into UPI in 1958, so stamp combinations precisely date late-50s photos. **Kodachrome slides peak in the 1950s** — red-bordered cardboard mounts with month/year codes — and slides of roadside Americana, neon, gas stations, named celebrities, or identified locations bring **$8–$30 each routinely and $30–$200 for the best**.

**Hollywood is the decade's runaway category**. Marilyn Monroe original studio stamps run $50–$300; James Dean, Audrey Hepburn, Liz Taylor, Brando, Grace Kelly, and Elvis (1954–56 emergence) all command $25–$200 for vintage originals. **Elvis 1956 Wertheimer negatives sold for $30,596 at RR Auction**. Look for snipe captions naming specific films (*The Seven Year Itch*, *Rebel Without a Cause*) and for Bert Six or Frank Powolny photographer credits. **Sports Type 1s explode**: a 1951 Mantle Type 1 brought $843,750 in 2024, Willie Mays rookie-era runs $100–$800, Hank Aaron 1954 debut $150–$500, a 1959 Cassius Clay Type 1 sold for $17,400, and even role-player Type 1s clear $25–$100. **Bettie Page Klaw originals** (1952–57) sell at $50–$400; authentic ones have Irving Klaw's handwritten ink inventory numbers on the negative itself.

The 1950s **roadside Americana market is white-hot**. Generic motels run $8–$15; identified motels with neon, branded gas stations, and named diners hit $15–$50; **Las Vegas pre-1960 night neon, Route 66 specific locations, Disneyland 1955–59, and identified drive-in marquees** reach $50–$200. **Hot rods, chopped customs, and dragsters** with cars visible go $15–$75, named cars from car magazines $50–$300. **Railroad transition-era photos** — last steam runs, named diesels, depot RPPCs — bring $15–$100. **Early civil rights press photos are sleeper gold** — Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56), Little Rock Nine (1957), Emmett Till coverage (1955), and any Alabama small-town integration material from local newspaper morgues commands $40–$500 in Type 1. **Music Type 1s of Miles Davis, Coltrane, Monk, Billie Holiday, Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, BB King, and Hank Williams** clear $50–$300 each. Atomic test press photos (Nevada Test Site 1951–58) are an underrated $25–$200 niche.

## The 1960s

The 1960s introduces **NASA, Vietnam, civil rights at its peak, the British Invasion, and Type 1 press photography at its commercial zenith**. Press photos and 8×10 publicity glossies are still the bread-and-butter formats above $8; color Kodacolor snapshots arrive but are mostly worthless unless they show a vintage car, a Disneyland opening-era ride, a named celebrity, or an identifiable Alabama location.

**Music is the decade's runaway hot category**. **Beatles Type 1 press photos** routinely bring $50–$250, with Cavern or Ed Sullivan-era prints hitting $400–$1,000+; signed Beatles real photos with credible COAs have sold for **$15,000–$143,000**. Rolling Stones, Hendrix, Doors, Janis Joplin, Grateful Dead Type 1s run $40–$200; **Jim Marshall vintage silver-gelatin prints** signed by the photographer reach $800–$5,000+, with his Hendrix Monterey guitar-burning vintage prints hitting $3,000–$10,000+. Dylan, Cash, Motown acts run $25–$150. Jazz Type 1s of Monk, Coltrane, Miles, and Mingus clear $40–$200; named-photographer jazz silver prints $200–$2,000.

**Hollywood and TV** values are driven by Marilyn Monroe's final two years (Type 1 $150–$800; George Barris signed "Last Photos" $1,500–$5,000+), James Dean (still circulating, $40–$300), Steve McQueen, Sean Connery's Bond era, Star Trek 1966–69 original NBC publicity ($20–$80 with verified verso markings, but watch for the flood of $5–$15 modern reprints), the original *Batman*, and *Twilight Zone*. **Vietnam** combat color snapshots are mostly $2–$8 in bulk, but jump to $15–$50 when a soldier is named with unit and base on the back. **Eddie Adams's "Saigon Execution"** period AP wire prints reach $200–$800; later signed Adams prints have hit $43,200 at Swann.

**Civil rights is the decade's most important Alabama-specific category**. MLK Type 1s run $40–$300, with Selma/Birmingham/Montgomery-specific events at $75–$500. **Edmund Pettus Bridge / Bloody Sunday 1965** Type 1s reach $100–$500+. **Bull Connor Birmingham 1963 fire hoses and police dogs** sit at $75–$400. Malcolm X Type 1s $100–$600+. Spider Martin (Selma), Charles Moore (civil rights), Ernest Withers (Memphis/civil rights), and Gordon Parks named-photographer prints are top-tier. The Alabama newspaper morgue trade is the sleeper here — any photo with a verso stamp from the Birmingham News, Montgomery Advertiser, or Mobile Register and a civil rights subject is $20–$200 minimum.

**Sports Type 1s** of Mantle ($200–$3,000+ common, with the 1951 Bowman/1952 Topps source images selling for $50,000–$375,000), Clemente, Aaron, Koufax, Mays ($150–$1,500), and **Cassius Clay/Muhammad Ali 1964–71** ($50–$500, with iconic fight photos $300–$2,000+) define the category. The **1968 Mexico City Smith/Carlos black-power salute** Type 1 brings $200–$800+. **NASA Red Number Apollo originals** run $40–$400 for routine images and $600–$3,000+ for iconic frames; Mercury and Gemini Red Numbers are $50–$300, more for Glenn, Shepard, and Grissom. **Pre-1972 muscle cars** (GTO '64, Mustang '65, Camaro '67, Charger '68) factory press 8×10s run $15–$75; identifiable Kodachromes of muscle cars at dealerships with named drivers run $5–$25 per slide, with lots of 20+ reaching $100–$500.

The 1960s sleepers are **suburban racial-integration era snapshots, pre-Stonewall gay bar candids, Disneyland family snapshots from 1960–69 of opening-era attractions, Vietnam soldier snapshots with full back identification, Alabama-specific Wallace and Bear Bryant photographs, and Talladega Superspeedway opening-era images** (the track opened 1969). Original photographer's contact sheets are a quiet $100–$500+ category.

## The 1970s

The 1970s continues press photography at high volume but transitions to **classic rock, the fall of Saigon, Watergate, disco, Studio 54, and the emergence of punk and proto-hip-hop**. Press photos remain gold standard; color press photos appear more frequently and command $15–$75 when subjects are strong. **The SX-70 Polaroid arrives in 1972** and develops a cult collector market — anonymous moody SX-70s sell as decorator art at $10–$30, but **any SX-70 of an identifiable celebrity, party scene, or named photographer's work** jumps to $200–$1,000+. Andy Warhol Polaroids have sold from $500 to $24,000+.

**Music drives the decade's top prices**. Led Zeppelin, Pink Floyd, Bowie, Queen, Elton John, Fleetwood Mac, Eagles Type 1s run $30–$200; **photographer-attributed vintage prints by Mick Rock (Bowie), Neal Preston, Bob Gruen, or Jim Marshall** reach $300–$3,000+. **Punk and new wave** late-70s photos are explosive: Sex Pistols, Ramones, Clash, Blondie, Patti Smith, Television, Talking Heads Type 1s clear $50–$400, with **Bob Gruen and Roberta Bayley originals at $400–$3,000** and CBGB-era photos especially hot. **Studio 54 candids** (Bianca Jagger, Halston, Liza, Warhol) by Bill Bernstein, Anton Perich, Adam Scull, or Ron Galella run $50–$500+. **Early hip-hop 1977–79 sleepers** — Grandmaster Flash, Kool Herc, Sugarhill Gang, Bronx party flyers and candids — are appreciating fastest of any category, with vintage photographs reaching $100–$2,000+.

**Hollywood blockbusters define the studio publicity market**: original *Star Wars* 1977 studio-marked 8×10s $25–$150, *Jaws*, *Godfather*, and *Rocky* originals $10–$50, **blaxploitation** (Shaft, Superfly, Foxy Brown, Pam Grier) $20–$150, Bruce Lee Hong Kong studio stills $50–$500, original SNL 1975 cast publicity (Belushi, Aykroyd, Radner) $30–$200. **Vietnam coverage** peaks with **Nick Ut's "Napalm Girl" June 8, 1972** period wire prints at $200–$1,000 and signed Ut prints $500–$5,000; fall of Saigon Type 1s $75–$400; My Lai Ronald Haeberle prints $200–$1,500.

**Sports Type 1s** of Hank Aaron's 715th home run April 8, 1974 bring $100–$800; Ali's "Fight of the Century" 1971, Rumble in the Jungle 1974, and Thrilla in Manila 1975 each hit $75–$1,000+; **1972 Munich Olympics Israeli hostage crisis** Type 1s clear $30–$500. **NASCAR Type 1s of Petty, Pearson, and Yarborough — especially Talladega-specific** — run $25–$150 and tap directly into the Alabama collector base.

**News events** include Watergate (Nixon resignation Aug 9, 1974 Type 1 $50–$300), Patty Hearst/SLA 1974 ($30–$200), Jonestown Nov 1978 ($30–$200), Three Mile Island 1979 ($20–$100), Iran hostage crisis ($20–$150), Elvis death Aug 16, 1977 ($30–$200), and Manson trials ongoing ($40–$250). **LGBTQ post-Stonewall Pride march photos** clear $40–$250; **Harvey Milk Type 1s** (1977–78) hit $75–$500; drag balls and gay bar candids with identifiable subjects $25–$200. **Amtrak forms May 1971**, making any color slide of a *last run* of the Super Chief, Broadway Limited, or 20th Century Limited before May 1 worth $10–$50.

## The 1980s

The 1980s is the **type-1 sports decade par excellence and the start of the modern celebrity Polaroid market**. Most family color snapshots, drugstore prints, and disposable camera output are worthless; the value sits narrowly but deeply in press photos, music industry promo 8×10s, signed authentics, and identifiable celebrity Polaroids. Polaroid 600 film launches in 1981; SX-70 production winds down by 1988. By 1995 Getty Images appears as a stamp — that single fact dates the photo.

**Sports Type 1 is the decade's runaway market**. **Michael Jordan 1984–86 rookie-era Type 1s** run $500–$10,000+, with a Jordan NBA debut Type 1 selling at **$225,000** and a signed UNC Type 1 at $7,930. Magic Johnson and Larry Bird $50–$500. **Wayne Gretzky 1979–80 rookie Type 1s** have sold $11,000–$43,920 documented; 1980s era $100–$1,500. **Mike Tyson amateur** rare Junior Olympics Type 1 hit $20,740; 1985–88 pro Type 1s $75–$500. Cal Ripken, NFL stars (Marino, Montana, Elway, L.T.) $25–$200.

**Music** delivers Madonna Type 1 press 1983–85 ($50–$300+), Michael Jackson Thriller era ($50–$500), Prince Purple Rain ($40–$200), **Run-DMC, Public Enemy, NWA, LL Cool J — early hip-hop Type 1s appreciating fastest of any category** ($50–$400+, with **Chi Modu and Janette Beckman attributed prints into the thousands**). Hair metal (Mötley Crüe, GNR, Poison, Bon Jovi) promo 8×10s $15–$75. Record label promo photos with Warner Bros., Geffen, or Def Jam back stamps carry a $10–$30 floor. **Princess Diana Type 1s** generally $15–$80, with one unpublished Caren Archive image hitting $18,306 as an extreme outlier. **ACT UP and AIDS-crisis** press photos $20–$150; identified Pride parade figures (Marsha P. Johnson, Harvey Fierstein) much higher. **1980s skate photography** is a real sleeper — Powell Peralta-era scene snapshots $20–$100, with **J. Grant Brittain and Glen E. Friedman attributed prints $50–$500+**. WWF Hulkamania Type 1 ringside $20–$100.

## The 1990s

The 1990s is **the type-1 hip-hop decade and the last full decade of physical press photography** before digital killed the market around 2005. Tupac, Biggie, and Nirvana define the top end; OJ, Princess Diana, and supermodels fill the middle. By the late 1990s most amateur output is on resin-coated drugstore paper that has zero collector value.

**Tupac and Biggie Type 1s** routinely run $75–$500+ for common images and **into the thousands** for iconic or photographer-attributed; Chi Modu's Biggie WTC image hit **$30,240 at auction**. Nirvana Type 1 press 1991–94 runs $50–$400; signed authentic Cobain $1,000+. **Tiger Woods 1996+ rookie-era Type 1** ranges $200–$2,000 normally; the source image for his Upper Deck rookie card sold for **$284,712**. **Kobe Bryant 1996–99 rookie-era** $200–$3,000. McGwire/Sosa 1998 home run chase $25–$150. **Serena and Venus Williams pre-pro Type 1**: a single childhood photo sold $10,370. Brett Favre, Cal Ripken Sept 1995 streak $20–$200.

**OJ Simpson 1994–95 Type 1** (Bronco chase, courtroom, glove) is a surprisingly active $15–$75 market, currently lifted by renewed interest. Oklahoma City 1995 $25–$100; Princess Diana funeral 1997 $30–$150; Columbine 1999 $20–$100 (sells quietly); Rodney King/LA Riots 1992 $25–$100; Waco 1993 $25–$100. **Supermodels (Crawford, Schiffer, Moss, Campbell, Evangelista) Type 1** $25–$150, with **Herb Ritts and Steven Meisel attributed prints much higher**. *Pulp Fiction*, *Titanic*, and Friends cast publicity $15–$60. **Early Apple/Microsoft press** (Jobs/Gates) $20–$100 and rising. **Selena Quintanilla Type 1** $40–$200 with a strong Texas/Southwest base. Spice Girls, Backstreet Boys, *NSYNC, Britney late-90s promo 8×10s $8–$30.

## The 2000s

The 2000s is the **death of physical press photography** — and that scarcity is the story. By 2005–2008 virtually all news photography was digital and printed on demand, making **true silver-gelatin or chromogenic originals from 2000–2010 rarer than 1980s prints**. Type 1 designation matters more than ever because most "press photos" from this era are actually inkjet reproductions worth almost nothing.

**Sports Type 1s** drive top-end value: **LeBron James 2003+ rookie-era** Type 1 $200–$3,000+, with high-school Type 1s even higher; **Tom Brady 2001–04 early Patriots** Type 1 $50–$500; Kobe peak 2000–10 $50–$500; Jeter and Pujols $25–$200; Tiger continues at $50–$500. **9/11 photographs are a significant market**: original Type 1 press photos from September 11, 2001 run $30–$300, with named-photographer originals (Thomas E. Franklin "Flag Raising at Ground Zero," Stan Honda, Suzanne Plunkett) reaching $100–$1,000; amateur prints showing the towers with verifiable lab date stamps on back command premiums; ground-zero workers and missing-person flyers $15–$75. Note that digital reprints of 9/11 imagery are everywhere — Type 1 distinction is essential.

**Music Type 1s** of Eminem, 50 Cent, Jay-Z, Kanye early-2000s $30–$200; White Stripes, Strokes indie rock $15–$75; emo (MCR, FOB, Paramore) $10–$40; OutKast Stankonia era $20–$100; Beyoncé/Destiny's Child early Type 1s $20–$100. **Reality TV stars** are a quiet appreciating market: Paris Hilton 2003–07 Type 1 $15–$75; **pre-fame Kim Kardashian Polaroids and snapshots ~2006** $20–$100+ and climbing.

**Hurricane Katrina 2005** Type 1 press is **especially valuable for an Alabama seller** — Gulf Coast destruction in Mississippi and Alabama specifically clears $25–$150 because of regional collecting interest. **Deepwater Horizon 2010** Gulf-relevant photos $15–$75. Iraq War "Mission Accomplished" Type 1 $20–$100; Afghanistan combat photojournalism $25–$150; Saddam capture and statue toppling 2003 $25–$100; Obama 2008 election Type 1 $20–$100 with iconic images higher.

## Categories that span the entire 1940–2010 period

Several niches hold value across every decade and represent the easiest finds for an attentive sorter. **Occupational photographs** of people posed with the tools of their trade — blacksmith with anvil, telephone operator at switchboard, photographer with camera, fireman with helmet, miner with lamp, lineman on pole, female welder, soda jerk, milkman — run $20–$200+ for snapshots and $40–$300+ for tintypes and cabinet cards. **Storefronts and visible signage** with readable brands (Coca-Cola, Texaco, Sinclair, Rexall, Woolworth's) consistently bring $20–$80; **identifiable Alabama small-town main streets** clear $15–$75+ across all eras with Birmingham, Mobile, Montgomery, Selma, Tuscaloosa, and Huntsville-NASA material especially strong. **LGBTQ vintage** photographs of affectionate same-sex couples, drag, and pre-Stonewall scenes have an established and growing institutional market at $25–$250+. **Black Americana** of dignified family, business, military, and church subjects has surging demand from HBCUs and the Smithsonian NMAAHC at $25–$500+ (handle racist material with extreme care — reputable resellers either decline it or sell only to educational institutions). **Halloween costumes, eerie/uncanny snapshots, circus and sideshow performers, tattooed subjects, train wrecks, disaster scenes, and named Native American subjects** all cross decade boundaries at $30–$300+.

## A practical sorting workflow for the Alabama box

Set up with a 10× jeweler's loupe (non-negotiable), an LED magnifying lamp, the stamp box dating chart printed out, archival sleeves, and a phone with eBay's sold-listing filter ready. First pass: sort purely by format into twelve piles — RPPCs, printed postcards, press 8×10s, snapshots, CDV/cabinet/tintype, slides by mount era, Polaroids by film type, negatives, studio movie/TV stills, NASA, stereoviews, and damage candidates. Second pass: inspect every photo using ten rules.

The most valuable habits are these:

- **Flip every single photo over before making any value decision.** Backs hold roughly half the value information — stamps, snipes, dates, photographer credits, hand-written names, agency markings, and drugstore date stamps.
- **Loupe every suspected RPPC** for the stamp box, and every suspected press photo for the snipe and wire-transmission scan lines. Halftone-dot rosette on the front under loupe disqualifies the image as a real photograph.

Reject quickly when you see: generic family snapshots with no identification and no back markings; modern resin-coated paper on a "vintage" subject (= reprint); inkjet dot patterns under loupe; perfectly uniform borders with no aging; preprinted signatures (identical, flat, no ink topography); 4×6 disposable camera prints; 1990s–2000s home printer output. Keep ANY photo with writing on the back for second-pass evaluation, even if the front looks unremarkable — a boring portrait with "Bessemer Steel, 1947, Robert L., Local 1013" on the back is a $50 photo to a labor historian.

For Alabama provenance, always second-check anything showing or labeled Birmingham (Sloss Furnaces, 16th Street Baptist Church, Vulcan, steel mills, civil rights), Tuscaloosa (Bama football, Bear Bryant, Stillman College), Mobile (Mardi Gras, port, USS *Alabama*), Montgomery (Dexter Avenue Church, bus boycott, Hank Williams), Selma (Edmund Pettus Bridge), Huntsville (Marshall Space Flight Center, Werner von Braun, Redstone Arsenal, Saturn V era), Anniston (Freedom Riders bus burning), Tuskegee (Institute, Airmen, syphilis study era — handle with care), Talladega (Superspeedway 1969+), Muscle Shoals (FAME and Muscle Shoals Sound studio musicians), and any small Alabama town with identified storefronts. State archives, the Birmingham Public Library, University of Alabama's Hoole Library, and regional collectors maintain consistent demand.

Listing strategy: photograph the BACK alongside the front in every listing — the verso *is* the provenance. Use keywords aggressively in titles ("Type 1," "Press Photo," "Original," "RPPC," "Kodachrome," "Real Photo," "Vintage," "Vernacular Photography") combined with subject + decade + location. For Tier 1 photos sell in lots of 20–50 to clear bulk at $15–$30/lot; for Tier 2–3 list individually with multiple high-resolution photos; for Tier 4+ consider consigning to Heritage, Goldin, Hake's, Britannic Auctions, RR Auction, or Cowan's, and submit clear Type 1 candidates to PSA/DNA (cost $50–$200) when the subject justifies it.

The defining insight across all seven decades is consistent: **the back is more important than the front, identification beats age, and crossover collector appeal stacks value**. The reseller who flips every photo, knows the stamp boxes, recognizes Type 1 markers, and aggressively pulls Southern provenance will routinely find $50–$500 photos in boxes that other dealers liquidated for $20 the whole lot.
