# The AlabamaCollects postcard reselling field guide

**Bottom line up front:** Your real money lives in **RPPCs and small-town views from Alabama and the South**, sold as singles on eBay with keyword-dense 80-character titles, while everything common rolls through weekly themed Whatnot $1-start shows. The $5–$50 everyday band is built on four pillars — Real Photo Postcards of identifiable subjects, small-town topographicals, holiday cards (especially Halloween), and roadside Americana — with crossover postal-history details (Doane cancels, DPOs, RPOs) acting as a price multiplier. Your Lineville sourcing radius is structurally undersupplied to the national market; treat that as your moat. The rest of this guide is how to convert it.

---

## 1. Eras and how to date what you're holding

Dating drives keywords, which drive search visibility, which drives price. Work the back first, the front second.

**Pioneer (pre-July 1898)** — Backs say "Souvenir Card," "Mail Card," "Correspondence Card," **never "Post Card"** (that phrase was reserved for government postals). Undivided backs, often vignette/multi-view "gruss aus" style. Genuinely scarce — common views **$50–$300**, exposition or unusual subjects **$500–$2,000+**. Most "pioneer" cards in estate lots aren't actually pioneer; verify before you celebrate.

**Private Mailing Card (May 19, 1898 – Dec. 23, 1901)** — The single phrase **"PRIVATE MAILING CARD — AUTHORIZED BY ACT OF CONGRESS ON MAY 19, 1898"** dates the card to this exact window. Undivided back; message had to be written on the front. Common views **$8–$25**, better topical/holiday/exposition PMCs **$25–$150+**. Add the keyword "PMC" to titles — collectors search it.

**Undivided Back / Post Card era (Dec. 24, 1901 – March 1, 1907)** — "POST CARD" appears on the back, but the back is still address-only and messages run across the front of the image. German chromolithography peaks here; Detroit Publishing introduces Phostint in 1903. Common views $3–$10, German chromolithos $10–$40, early Clapsaddle/Tuck $25–$100+.

**Divided Back / Golden Age (March 1, 1907 – c. 1915)** — The U.S. adopted the divided back on March 1, 1907 (UK 1902, France 1903, Germany 1905). The hobby explodes: **668 million postcards mailed in the U.S. in fiscal 1908; ~900 million by 1913.** Most period cards are German-printed, often marked "Made in Germany," "Printed in Saxony," or "Printed in Bavaria" — that wording effectively disappears with the 1914–15 import collapse. This is the **sweet spot** for everyday $5–$50 finds. Key publishers: **Raphael Tuck & Sons (Oilette series), John Winsch, Detroit Publishing, International Art Publishing, Paul Finkenrath of Berlin (PFB), Ernest Nister, Whitney Made, Bamforth, Stecher Litho.**

**White Border (c. 1915 – 1930)** — White margin saved ink during the post-WWI American printing transition. Notably **lower quality** than German Golden Age work — flatter colors, coarser halftones, many are just American reprints of earlier designs with borders added. Curt Teich, Detroit Publishing, Tichnor, Acmegraph, Asheville Postcard dominate. Common $1–$5; identifiable small-town views $5–$25.

**Linen (c. 1930 – 1945, lingering to ~1955)** — Textured fabric-look surface, gaudy candy-colored dye inks. **Curt Teich's "C.T. Art-Colortone"** dominates; competitors include Tichnor, Colourpicture, Asheville Postcard, MWM, E.C. Kropp, Metrocraft. This is the heartland of **roadside Americana**. Common $1–$5, roadside/advertising/Black Americana/large-letter $5–$25, rare advertising or Curt Teich oddities $25–$100+.

**Photochrome / Chrome (1939 – present)** — Glossy color photo-offset. **Mike Roberts' WESCO cards for Union Oil in 1939** marked the start. Dexter Press, Plastichrome, Curteichcolor, Mike Roberts dominate. Bulk chromes are $0.25–$2 each; lot them aggressively. Exceptions for niche subjects (Route 66, specific motels, baseball stadiums, civil rights, early Disney, named neon signs) can hit $25–$300+.

**Continental size (4" × 6")** became dominant in the U.S. in the 1970s; standard size before was 3.5" × 5.5". **A 4×6 card is almost always 1970s+** — useful repro detector when the design looks pre-1920.

### Real Photo Postcards (RPPCs): the single most important format to identify

True photographic prints on postcard-stock photo paper, enabled by Kodak's 1903 #3A Folding Pocket Kodak and the AZO postcard-back paper. **Under a 10× loupe:** RPPCs show continuous photographic tone with no dot pattern and often slight silver-mirror sheen at the edges. Printed cards show a rosette halftone dot pattern. RPPCs typically sold in tiny runs — dozens to a few hundred per image — which is why they routinely outvalue printed equivalents **2–10×**, and for unique subjects 50–100×.

**The stamp box dates the paper, not the exposure** — combine with image clues (cars, fashion, signage, postmark) to narrow further. A KODAK stamp box on a "1910" Main Street scene is a 1950s+ reprint.

### Quick dating decision tree

1. Loupe — RPPC or printed? If RPPC → check stamp box.
2. Front text — "Souvenir Card" / "Mail Card" → pre-1898. "Private Mailing Card" → 1898–1901.
3. Back layout — Undivided with "Post Card" wording → 1901–1907 (check front for writing). Divided → 1907+ in U.S.
4. Surface — White margin → 1915–1930. Linen weave + candy color → 1930–1945. Glossy → 1939+.
5. Size — 3.5×5.5 → pre-1970s. 4×6 → 1970s+.
6. Postage clues — 1¢ rate windows: May 1898–Nov 1917, Jul 1919–Apr 1925, Jun 1928–Dec 1951. 2¢ rate windows: **Nov 1917–Jun 1919 (WWI surcharge)**, Apr 1925–Jun 1928, and from Jan 1952. ZIP codes → July 1963+. Two-digit postal zones ("Chicago 7, Ill.") → 1943–1963.
7. Publisher serial — **Curt Teich code "X-Y####" letters: A=1930s, B=1940s, C=1950s, D=1960s, E=1970s; preceding digit = year-of-decade** (so 2B-H446 = 1942). Detroit Publishing pre-1932 cards often carry small Roman numerals near the dividing line (xvii = 1917, xx = 1920).

## 2. What collectors actually want — the desirability map

This section is the heart of the guide. Specific subjects, specific price bands, specific keywords.

### Topographical views — the spine of the hobby

Deltiology, systematic postcard collecting, is built on town view collectors. Many collectors pursue every card from one town, county, or state. **Smaller and more obscure towns outsell big cities** because print runs were tiny, survivors are rare, and hometown / historical-society buyers create a constant demand floor. A 1910 Times Square view sells for $1–$3 today; a 1910 Main Street view of a 500-person Kansas town sells for $30–$100, and as an RPPC, $75–$300+.

Practical rule of thumb by town population (1910 census):
- Over 50,000 — mass-produced, low scarcity, usually under $5 unless RPPC of unusual subject.
- 5,000–50,000 — moderate scarcity, $5–$25 typical.
- 500–5,000 — scarce, $15–$75 typical for identified Main Street views.
- Under 500 — rare, $30–$150+.
- Ghost town / DPO — rare to very rare, $50–$300+ with postal-history crossover.

**Main Street RPPCs** are the prize within the prize. Per Playle's pricing guide, a clear identified pre-1940 Main Street RPPC starts around **$25–$45** and adds: **+$25 for D&H (dirt street with horses), +$10 for visible trolley, +$10 for legible storefront/advertising sign, +$30 for Southern state, –$5 for Northern state, –66% if blurred or unidentified.** Watch for legible brand signage — Coca-Cola, Moxie, Keen Kutter, Ford, Dr Pepper, Gold Dust Twins, VIN FIZ each add roughly $10. Identifiable early autos add value, as do identified photographers and horse-drawn vehicles.

**Civic buildings** form a recognizable hierarchy. **School interiors** ($15–$50 RPPC) outpace exteriors ($7–$20). **Courthouses** routinely $12–$35, identified small-county courthouses $25–$50. **Train depots** are $15–$45 sweet spot for identified small-town stations, with massive crossover from railroadiana collectors; the Seabrook TX Southern Pacific depot RPPC sold $1,255. Post offices generally low ($4–$8) — too many were produced. Libraries, Masonic temples, Elks halls $10–$25. City halls and fire stations $12–$25, with fire stations showing horse-drawn equipment running $125–$175 RPPC.

### Roadside Americana — the linen era's heartland

Curt Teich and competitors mass-produced linens of the new auto-tourist landscape from 1930–1945. **Gas stations** with branded porcelain signage hit $5–$25 linen and $50–$165 RPPC — Texaco, Standard Oil, Sinclair (dinosaur), Phillips 66, Mobil/Mobilgas (Pegasus), Esso, Conoco, Gulf, Pure Oil, Tydol, Cities Service all carry premiums. **Motels, motor courts, tourist courts** $5–$15 typical, $15–$30 for themed properties (Wigwam Village, Blue Swallow, Munger Moss, El Rancho), $20–$50 for Route 66 corridor. **Diners and drive-ins** $5–$25; A&W, Howard Johnson's, classic stainless diners $15–$40; White Castle $20–$60 (dedicated chain collectors). **Route 66 specifically** lifts even unidentified linen/chrome values — recognizing the geography is your skill since many cards don't say "Route 66."

### Transportation

**Railroad** is the biggest crossover category in collecting. Depots covered above; named trains (20th Century Limited, Super Chief, Empire State Express) carry premiums; train wrecks $20–$45 typical; trolleys/streetcars/interurbans $25–$45; defunct shortline depots draw specialists. A trolley visible in a town view adds about $10 to the base view value.

**Ships** — common ocean liners $5–$30, but **Titanic** is its own category: pre-disaster mailing cards and memorial "Most Appalling Disaster" cards $50–$300+; **sailing-card postcards mailed FROM the Titanic run in thousands**. Lusitania $15–$100; silk Lusitania embroidered $75–$200. Great Lakes freighters, lightships ($125–$200 RPPC), and ferries each have specialist collector bases.

**Aviation** — early biplanes / air shows RPPC $50–$125, commercial aviation pre-1920 RPPC $200–$400, airships/Zeppelins $125–$175, female aviator RPPC $200–$500. **Wright Brothers RPPCs $100–$10,000+** (1909 College Park aeroplane sold $10,000).

**Bridges, dams, lighthouses, windmills** — covered bridges RPPC $8–$12, **lighthouses** $35–$100 RPPC base / $50–$200+ identified historic (active specialist base), dams generally low except Hoover construction RPPCs $20–$60, Burgess-publisher bridge cards $600+.

### Holidays — the value hierarchy

**Halloween dwarfs everything else.** Standard cards $5–$75 by image quality; artist-signed (Clapsaddle, Brundage, Tuck) $20–$200; **John Winsch publisher cards (1911–1915) $40–$200+**, often with Samuel Schmucker art; **Schmucker designs $100–$700+ individually**, with the **Winsch "Mask Set" reaching $1,476** and the "White-on-White" set $1,353. **Hold-to-Light Halloween $75–$800+**, mechanical Halloween $150–$500+. Keywords that move Halloween: JOL, jack-o'-lantern, witch, black cat, owl, devil, goblin, Pierrot, embossed, Germany-printed.

**Christmas / Santa** is the second-tier holiday but has its own outliers. Common red-suit Santas $3–$25; **colored-suit Santas are the play** — blue, green, purple, brown, yellow robe Santas $40–$700+, with one HTL blue Santa cataloged at $719. **Krampus** runs $25–$300+ originals, $200–$1,000+ for embossed/Mailick-signed examples (surging modern interest). HTL Christmas $30–$400+. **Uncle Sam Santa is the rarest Christmas subject** — $899 embossed to $1,876 HTL, even printed versions $100–$400.

**Thanksgiving** — common turkeys $3–$10; **Uncle Sam Thanksgiving $25–$100+** (patriotic crossover); artist-signed $10–$40.

**Valentine's Day** — common $2–$10, signed (Clapsaddle, Brundage, Tuck) $7–$30, mechanicals $20–$400+, HTL $50–$300+, **Schmucker Valentine "ladies" for Winsch $75–$500+**.

**Other holidays** in rough value order: **Leap Year** women-chasing-men comic theme $10–$35 (niche but active), **Fourth of July** patriotic embossed $8–$30 and Uncle Sam patriotic $20–$80, **Decoration Day** with GAR/soldiers $10–$35, **St. Patrick's** Clapsaddle Irish children $8–$25, **Easter** common $2–$8 (HTL Easter $47–$75, mechanical $20–$100), **New Year** common $2–$10 (Father Time figures $10–$30, round-year cards premium), **Groundhog Day** rare $30–$150+. Birthday is generally bulk ($0.50–$3) unless artist-signed.

### Black Americana / African American — handle with awareness

Two very different categories share this shelf and they should be approached differently. The cleanest, most ethically straightforward, and often most valuable segment is **RPPCs of identified African American families, businesses, churches, schools, fraternal lodges, and soldiers** — $30–$150 routine, $200–$700+ for exceptional content. Black-owned business RPPCs, Black doctors, ministers, professional photographs, jazz/circus bands ($100–$250), and **Tuskegee Institute** material all fit here. **This market is growing**, particularly for cards documenting Black middle-class life.

The **derogatory caricature** segment has documented market values but ethical concerns: minstrel/Kemble stereotype cards $5–$40 common, rare designs $25–$100. **eBay restricts overtly racist content and explicitly bans lynching postcards**; do not list them. KKK material is similarly restricted. Reference: J.L. Mashburn, *Black Americana Postcard Price Guide* (1996). When in doubt about a card, the safe path is factual neutral captioning or routing it to a museum/archive rather than a collector channel.

### Other categories worth knowing

**Patriotic and military** — embossed patriotic Golden Age $5–$25, better designs $30–$80, Uncle Sam RPPC $50–$150. WWI training camp RPPCs $15–$50 (huge supply); silk embroidered "sweetheart" cards $5–$75 common but $30–$300+ for rare regimental crests. WWII home front linens $5–$25, named bases/ships $20–$60.

**Advertising** — generic Coca-Cola postcards $9–$40, but the **Hamilton King "Coca-Cola Girl" 1909–1910 $200–$1,500**; **Coca-Cola truck RPPC $500–$2,600, wagon RPPC $1,800–$3,000**. Patent medicine wagons (Watkins, Raleigh's, Pinkham's) $100–$800. Cigar store interior RPPCs $175–$220. Departmental chain views (Woolworth's, Kress, McCrory's, Newberry's, Murphy's, Ben Franklin) $5–$25 with sign visible.

**Novelty formats** — Hold-to-Light $35–$80 starter / $200–$800+ for top subjects (the dealer rule of thumb is "$1 per hole"). Mechanical $20–$150 / $400+ for German Halloween. Add-ons (real hair, feathers, glitter, fabric) $10–$40 common / $75–$250 elaborate, with intact original material being condition-critical. **Large Letter "Greetings From" $5–$30** routinely, with state cards most common; "Greetings from [small City]" $40–$100. Fantasy/exaggeration (giant fish, corn — William H. Martin of Ottawa KS is the famous name) $15–$50 / $75–$200+ for unusual subjects.

**Political / social history** — **Suffrage is hot**: pro-suffrage commercial $30–$150, anti-suffrage Dunston-Weiler 12-card series $40–$125 each / complete sets $500+, Kewpie suffrage carries double-crossover premium. Teddy Roosevelt RPPC $40–$65, Lincoln memorial $15–$50. Prohibition Party RPPC $250–$300. **Socialist Party wagon NYC $2,500–$3,600**; Eugene Debs RPPC $1,000–$2,000.

**Sports** is where outliers happen. **Identified Negro Leagues baseball $4,500–$25,000**, identified player RPPCs $500–$25,000+ (1911 Lincoln Giants $18,172; Walter Johnson $23,558; Babe Ruth $564,000 record). Even chrome-era baseball stadium cards can hit $100s. Boxing pro RPPC $50–$125 ($200–$350 Black boxing). Tattooed-lady sideshow RPPC $400–$3,000. Jim Thorpe Carlisle $1,000–$6,000.

**Native American** RPPCs of named individuals $50–$500+, Geronimo $50–$125, **Edward S. Curtis RPPCs $100–$400 each / sets $1,000–$5,000+**. Carlisle Indian School $30–$100. Generic linen "Indian" cards $2–$10.

**Niche crossovers** with their own active collector bases: occupational RPPCs (blacksmiths, dentists, cobblers, telephone operators, billboard hangers) $75–$350 / quilting bees $400–$500; school interiors $15–$50; covered bridges RPPC $8–$12; mining ghost towns $30–$500+; psychiatric/asylum interiors $20–$100; Alcatraz/Sing Sing/Eastern State prisons; breweries pre-Prohibition $25–$150 / wagon $100–$250; universities pre-1920 RPPC $25–$75; Klondike/Yukon $25–$200; bird's-eye/panoramic $20–$80 RPPC; disasters (floods, fires, tornadoes, train wrecks) $8–$20 routine / $20–$100+ for famous events with strong content.

**Foreign topographicals** are generally lower demand in the U.S. market unless they're (a) RPPCs of small villages, (b) **Asian pre-1950** (Shanghai, Tokyo, Hong Kong c. 1920s street scenes $50–$1,225+), (c) Mexican Revolution $30–$200, (d) colonial-era African/South American RPPCs $20–$150, or (e) target a country with an active collector base.

## 3. The value equation — why one card is $40 and another is $1

Value is **scarcity intersected with demand from specific collector bases**. The reseller's edge is recognizing cards that satisfy multiple bases simultaneously — that's where bidding wars happen.

**The big three value drivers, in order:** subject desirability, town/location specificity, and RPPC-versus-printed. Together they explain ~80% of value above $5.

**Crossover collector appeal is the secret multiplier.** An RPPC of a train depot in a tiny Kansas town with a Doane cancel attracts (1) railroad/depot collectors, (2) town-view collectors for that state, (3) Doane cancel specialists, (4) DPO collectors if the office is now closed, and (5) Kansas postal history collectors. Five buyer pools competing for one item turns a $15 card into a $75–$200 auction.

### Condition grading — the standard 7-grade deltiology scale

| Grade | Description | Price vs. Mint |
|---|---|---|
| **Mint (M)** | Perfect, looks just-printed | 1.0× baseline |
| **Near Mint (NM)** | Trace handling, sharp corners | 0.80–0.90× |
| **Excellent (EX)** | Clean image; postally used on back is OK | 0.65–0.80× |
| **Very Good (VG)** | Slightly blunt corners, almost-invisible crease | 0.45–0.60× |
| **Good (G)** | Noticeable corner wear, light crease | 0.25–0.40× |
| **Fair (F)** | Significant creases/stains/edge damage | 0.10–0.20× |
| **Poor (P)** | Torn, missing chunks | 0.05× |

Specific flaws to identify and disclose explicitly: corner wear (check all four under angled light), creasing (any white surface crack), album marks/indentations, edge chips, stains, **foxing** (brown rust-spots from fungal activity), writing on the front (major demerit except in 1901–1907 when it was required), cancel ink bleed-through, thinning from album removal (hold to light), pinholes, and **silvering** on RPPCs (technically degradation but it actually *confirms* authentic silver-halide RPPC, so don't downgrade it).

Rule of thumb: Mint commands roughly 2× of Very Good for the same card, ~4× of Good. For ultra-rare cards condition matters less; for common cards condition is everything.

### Used vs. unused — the rule that runs the game

Default: unused/mint sells slightly higher because the picture side is clean. **But** for RPPCs, town views, and topographicals, **postally used can ADD significant value** when the postmark offers postal-history interest. The postal history collector base is completely separate from picture collectors, and both bid on the same card.

**Cancels that add value:**

- **Doane Cancels (1903–1906, used into 1920s)** — early experimental rubber handstamps for small post offices. Three types: Type 1 (5 killer bars with number, 1903), **Type 2 (2 sets of railroad-track double bars with number, Sep 1903–Jun 1905, ~17,500 issued)**, and **Type 3 (4 solid bars with number, Jul 1905–Fall 1906, ~12,000 issued)**. The number indicates the postmaster's annual compensation tier; "1" = under $100/year, higher numbers are scarcer. Doane specialists chase town-by-town lists (doanecancel.com). **Reseller premium: $5–$15 on common cards, $20–$60+ if the Doane is from a DPO or rare combination.**
- **DPO (Discontinued Post Office)** — a post office that no longer exists, usually closed by RFD consolidation (which closed ~30,000 small POs after 1902) or by the town becoming a ghost. There are tens of thousands. A card both *depicting* and *postmarked from* a DPO is a double-hit.
- **RFD (Rural Free Delivery) cancels** — applied by rural carriers, often in purple indelible pencil with carrier initials. Pre-1904 POD-issued rubber stamps are scarcer; specialists chase these.
- **RPO (Railway Post Office) cancels** — mail sorted on moving trains. U.S. RPO service ran 1862–1977; cancels read like "CHICAGO & OMAHA R.P.O." with a train number. Streetcar RPOs ended 1929, boat RPOs mostly ended WWII. Mobile Post Office Society collects them. Pairing an RPO cancel with a railroad-subject image hits the trifecta.
- **Flag cancels, fancy/pictorial cancels, slogan cancels, "socked-on-the-nose" full-strike CDS, military/censor markings (APO/FPO/field post), auxiliary markings** (forwarded, postage due, "Return to Sender," registry), **tied stamps** (cancel strike crosses both stamp and card surface — required for premium philatelic pricing), and **wartime-rate usages** (correctly franked 2¢ domestic postcard from Nov 1917–Jun 1919 is a postal-history micro-niche).

**Message content** that adds value: mentions of historical events (1918 flu, Titanic news, election day, Lindbergh flight, war news), named senders/recipients of note, hometown news that ties to the image, death/mourning, love letters with named couples.

### Price tiers — what reliably falls where

- **Under $1 (bulk only):** modern chrome tourist views, generic linen big-city skylines, generic foreign views, damaged commons, generic birthday/get-well.
- **$1–$5:** average chrome, common linen with character, generic divided-back views without postal-history interest, common holidays unsigned in average condition.
- **$5–$15 (entry-level singles):** identifiable mid-size town views in EX condition, decent non-signed holidays, entry-level RPPCs of identifiable but non-rare subjects, common artist-signed in average condition, better linens of unusual subjects, common depot RPPCs of major railroad junctions.
- **$15–$50 (the reseller's sweet spot):** solid small-town RPPCs (clearly identified, EX–NM, town under 5,000 population), named artist-signed holidays (Clapsaddle, Brundage, Bernhardt Wall, common Tuck signed), good-condition non-Schmucker Halloween, small-town railroad depots, roadside Americana with brand signage, advertising postcards with real storefronts, common Doane cancels on decent images, large-letter "Greetings From" small towns.
- **$50–$150 (strong finds, pull these for individual auction listing):** small-town Main Street RPPCs with crossover features (D&H, signage, identified town under 1,000), occupational RPPCs with named workers/trades, disaster RPPCs with strong content, entry-level Schmucker Halloween, hold-to-light cards, exposition rarities, Black Americana RPPCs, named Native American RPPCs, EX Mucha.
- **$150–$500+ (premium tier):** top Schmucker designs, rare expositions, pioneer cards, exceptional RPPCs (named African American subjects, named famous people, top disasters), HTL Halloween in EX–NM, mechanical Clapsaddle, named early aviators.
- **$500–$5,000+ (museum tier):** sell through Swann, Morphy, Heritage, or specialist postcard auctioneers, **not eBay**.

### Reproductions — how to spot them

The repro plague hit Halloween hardest but now includes linens. Tells: bright optically-white paper, slick modern coating, sharp halftone dots under loupe on what should be RPPC, missing publisher/stamp-box info, muted vs. over-bright color, lighter "heft," flatter embossing than originals. Pre-1920 cards never used the single word "STAMP" on the box; modern repros often do. Watch for the **glued-repro scam** (reproduced front glued to a genuine vintage back) — check the card edge under raking light for thickness mismatch. A 4"×6" continental card with a "1910" design is a repro; originals were 3.5"×5.5". A KODAK stamp box on a pre-1920 subject is a 1950s+ reprint.

### The 5-second triage sequence (use while sorting bulk lots)

1. Loupe — RPPC vs. printed?
2. Era — stamp box / back layout.
3. Subject — hot topical list?
4. Place specificity — town named? Population? Landmarks?
5. Postal markings — Doane / RPO / RFD / DPO / flag / censor / foreign / tied stamp?
6. Condition — four-corner check, hold to light for thinning, raked light for creases.
7. Crossover hits — count separate collector bases. 1 = bulk. 2 = solid sale. **3+ = stop, research, list individually targeting all bases by name in the title.**

## 4. The Alabama angle — your structural moat

Most national postcard dealers concentrate in the Northeast, Midwest, and Pacific Northwest. **Alabama postcards — especially small-town RPPCs — were produced and mailed within the state and surface today primarily through Alabama estate sales, attics, and church liquidations.** That's exactly where AlabamaCollects sources. The supply mismatch is real: a scan of eBay's Alabama Topographical category shows roughly 4,000 active listings, of which only about 14 are RPPC-tagged. RPPCs are dramatically underrepresented relative to demand. **Birmingham (2,800+ active), Mobile (3,900+), and Montgomery (2,000+) are saturated for common views** — you compete on these only with RPPCs, unusual subjects, or DPO postmarks. Smaller towns are where margin lives.

Alabama also has 67 counties and thousands of historical post offices, many now DPOs. State-completionist deltiologists and county-history collectors are price-insensitive on hard-to-find towns. Genealogy/local-history Facebook groups, alumni, churches doing centennials, museums, and historical societies (Clay County Historical Society, Encyclopedia of Alabama, Auburn University Digital Library, Alabama Department of Archives & History) are repeat institutional and hometown buyers.

### Alabama trophy subjects

**Tier 1 (price aggressively):** **Sloss Furnaces** and Birmingham steel/TCI/Bessemer industrial Americana (a hot national category); **Vulcan Statue Birmingham** (world's-largest-statue + roadside crossover); **Wilson Dam, Wheeler Dam, Guntersville Dam (TVA)** with construction-era 1920s–30s RPPCs of Wilson Dam highly sought; **USS Alabama**; **Redstone Arsenal / Marshall Space Flight Center / NASA Huntsville / Saturn V / Wernher von Braun** (space collecting is a huge separate market); **Civil Rights era** (Selma, Edmund Pettus Bridge, 16th Street Baptist Birmingham, Dexter Avenue Church Montgomery, Rosa Parks Library); **Mobile Mardi Gras** (Mobile, not New Orleans, had the first U.S. Mardi Gras); **Bryant-Denny / Legion Field / Jordan-Hare** stadium views; **Talladega Superspeedway**; **Helen Keller / Ivy Green, Tuscumbia**; show caves (**Cathedral, DeSoto, Russell Cave**).

### Civil rights — sensitive but growing

Civil rights era cards (1955–1968) showing march routes, central churches (16th Street Baptist Birmingham, Brown Chapel AME Selma, Dexter Avenue King Memorial Montgomery), Edmund Pettus Bridge, memorials, and bus boycott sites have **rising institutional and academic demand**. For Confederate monument cards, factual neutral descriptions — many monuments have been removed, which has increased documentary demand. Avoid romanticizing the Lost Cause; avoid the racist caricature segment entirely.

### Southern regional crossovers

Cards in your supply will sell to broader Southern markets: Civil War sites (Vicksburg, Shiloh, Chickamauga, Lookout Mountain); cotton industry and plantations (sensitive); Mississippi/Tennessee/Alabama river riverboats; **TVA construction era (1933–1945)**; music heritage (**FAME Studios Muscle Shoals, Hank Williams Montgomery/Georgiana, W.C. Clay County has had many DPOs (RFD wiped out hundreds of small Alabama POs after 1902). Cross-reference doanecancel.com's Alabama coordinator lists. Your 1-hour sourcing radius covers Ashland, Anniston/Oxford/Jacksonville (wealthier estates), Talladega, Sylacauga (marble industry collectible niche), Alexander City, Wedowee/Roanoke/Lafayette, Gadsden, Heflin/Piedmont. ## 6. Listings, photography, and what to put in the description

Description must include, in this order: what's depicted (full visual description with building names, streets, costume details, brand signage); era/dating (divided vs. undivided, stamp box brand, postmark year, production code); publisher and printer (exact text from back, e.g., "Published by Curt Teich & Co., Chicago, C.T. American Art Colored, No. 7B-H1234"); type (RPPC, lithograph, linen, chrome, embossed, HTL, mechanical, silk, leather); dimensions (standard 3.5×5.5 vs. continental 4×6 — explicit to weed out repro questions); postmark details (city, date, stamp denomination, slogan/duplex cancel if interesting); **full verbatim transcription of the handwritten message in quotes** (critical for SEO — genealogists search ancestor names; many cards sell because someone's grandfather is named on the back); honest condition assessment using standard 7-grade vocabulary with every flaw disclosed; stock footer for shipping/returns/combined-shipping.

**Why publisher matters:** topical and artist collectors buy by publisher/artist. Knowing the difference between Detroit Publishing (early high-quality views), Curt Teich (linen/chrome; coded numbers date the card), Raphael Tuck (Oilette), John Winsch (premium embossed holiday with Schmucker art), Ellen Clapsaddle for International Art (most prolific Halloween artist), Whitney Made (holiday), Bamforth (UK comic), Rotograph, Albertype, Tichnor, E.C. Kropp, Hugh C. Leighton can swing a card from $2 to $50+.

**Photography rule #1: always shoot both sides.** The back is where postal historians, RPPC daters, publisher collectors, and message-readers look — buyers will not bid without seeing it. Front: flat top-down, fill the frame, natural diffused daylight or two soft LED panels at 45°, white/light-gray background, locked white balance (color accuracy is everything for hand-tinted and linen), no flash (kills embossing detail). For embossed cards, rake light from one side to show the embossing. Back: same lighting, frame to show stamp box, stamp/postmark, publisher imprint, message, and address; shoot a tight close-up if the postmark is dim.

**Scan vs. photograph:** A flatbed at 600 dpi (Epson V39/V600 is the workhorse) produces cleaner, more uniform results — many high-volume sellers scan exclusively. Trade-off: scans don't capture embossing/3-D well; photograph those. eBay allows 24 photos free; use them on $20+ cards. Shoot tight close-ups of any flaw — this prevents INAD returns by documenting condition upfront.

## 7. Selling strategy, pricing, and platforms

### Auction vs. fixed price vs. Best Offer

**Auction (7-day, $0.99 start, no reserve)** for rare/desirable cards where you don't know the ceiling — one-of-a-kind RPPCs, Halloween, pre-1910 holidays, anything you've never seen sell. End auctions **Sunday evening 7–10 PM Eastern**, the peak postcard buyer window. **Fixed Price / Buy It Now (30-day GTC)** for predictable, identifiable common-but-good cards — linens, chromes, common topicals — which is roughly 80% of $5–$50 inventory. **Fixed Price + Best Offer** is the sweet spot for the $20–$100 range; set auto-decline at 70% and auto-accept at 90%. Skip reserve auctions — fees and buyer aversion outweigh protection; use a higher starting bid instead.

### Lotting strategy — when to single, when to lot

Sell as singles when comps clear $5, the card is identifiable (publisher, town, artist), or it's RPPC/Halloween/holiday/Black Americana/occupational/transportation/military/advertising/anything pre-1915 in decent condition. Lot when per-card value is under $1–$2, damaged, unidentified, or generic.

| Mixed estate as-found | 100–500 | $25–$100 | $0.15–$0.30 |

| Themed by state | 25–50 | $20–$60 | $0.50–$1.50 |

**Pro tip: pull RPPCs and pre-1915 cards out of every estate-buy lot before lotting.** One $40 RPPC pays for the whole lot. Title themed lots with the theme front-loaded; photograph 6–12 representative cards in a grid rather than all 100; disclose "Condition varies, sold as-found, some duplicates/damaged."

### Pricing research — always check sold comps in this order

1. **eBay sold listings filter** — 90 days of completed-sale data, your primary source.
2. **eBay completed listings** — includes unsold; shows what didn't move at certain prices.
3. **Terapeak Product Research** — free with any eBay Store subscription ($4.95–$349.95/mo). Gives 1–2 year historical data, average sold price, sell-through rate. **Worth a Starter Store ($4.95/mo) just for Terapeak alone if you list ≥50/month.**
4. **HipPostcard sold/active** — niche, but pricing here often runs higher than eBay for serious collector-targeted items because the audience is purer.
5. **Delcampe** — the largest international postcard platform (Belgium-based); essential for European, world views, and rare cards over $20.
6. **WorthPoint** (paid ~$30/mo) — historical eBay/auction-house sold data going back ~15 years; useful for oddities.
7. **CardCow.com** — active dealer pricing; a retail-ceiling reference.
8. **Price guide books** — Mashburn's *Postcard Price Guide*, *Fantasy Postcards*, *Black Americana Postcard Price Guide*; Apkarian-Russell *Halloween Postcards Catalog*; Susan Brown Nicholson *Encyclopedia of Antique Postcards*; Q. David Bowers *Cordially Yours*; Jeffrey Meikle *Postcard America: Curt Teich and the Imaging of a Nation*.

### Whatnot live-selling tactics

$1-start auctions are the cultural norm and the algorithm rewards engagement; even cards that "lose" individually push the show up the feed and benefit your higher-value cards. Realistic pricing: **common postcards sell at 50–70% of eBay individual prices, but volume is 3–10× higher per hour**. $5–$15 cards average $6–$12 hammers in active shows. Halloween, Black Americana, and RPPCs of named towns can over-perform when collectors are in the room.

**Show formats that work:** themed shows (Halloween in October — gangbusters; Christmas in November/December; Valentine's in February; **Mardi Gras in February is gold for an Alabama seller**; state-themed nights; "Southern States" night; RPPC night). **Pick/shelf shows** — lay out 50–200 cards in sleeves, buyers buy picks at $3–$10 each. **Mystery/Surprise Sets** — Whatnot's blind feature, mixed sleeves of 5/10/20 cards at $10/$15/$25. **Lot breaks** — auction a sleeve of 25 random unsorted as one lot. **Buy-It-Now shop** — park $20+ singles in your Profile Shop at near-eBay prices for off-show sales.

Free giveaways every 15–30 minutes build followers but you pay shipping with no commission — use damaged commons only; never give away cards worth over $3. Don't auction your best card last; energy fades — mid-show is peak attention.

### Economics — the numbers behind a hobby

Sample $20 eBay single profit (using $0.25 sourcing): sale $20.00 + shipping $0.99 – FVF ~$2.78 – $0.30 order fee – $0.74 postage – $0.10 supplies – $0.25 sourcing = **~$16.82 net**. Same card on Whatnot: $20.00 + $1.00 shipping – $1.60 commission – $0.91 processing – $0.85 postage/supplies – $0.25 sourcing = **~$16.39 net**.

**Sourcing economics:** estate sale shoebox $10–$50 for 200–800 cards = $0.05–$0.25/card; thrift singles usually overpriced; antique mall booths $1–$5 (avoid unless clear win); flea market dealer lots $0.10–$0.50/card with negotiation; online lots $0.20–$0.80/card delivered.

**Time investment reality:** identifying, photographing, listing, and shipping a single card takes **8–15 minutes**. The card must clear ~$5 to justify minimum-wage labor cost. **Anything you can't comp at $5+ → lot it.** A 100-card lot takes about 30 minutes total and often sells for $25–$50 — much better $/hour than dribbling out commons.

**Realistic hobbyist income:** 5 hrs/week with ~30 listings/week and $8 average sale runs $200–$400/month gross, $120–$250 net. 15 hrs/week with ~100 listings/week mixing singles and lots runs $800–$1,500/month gross, $500–$1,000 net. 25+ hrs/week with an established Whatnot following and 1,000+ active eBay listings can hit $2,000–$5,000/month gross, $1,200–$3,500 net.

## 8. Pitfalls, sourcing tips, and where to learn more

**The pitfalls that cost new sellers money:** not photographing the back; vague titles ("Old Postcard Lot Nice Cards"); misgrading (calling VG "Excellent" — leads to returns and bad feedback); trying to PWE-ship cards with lumps/clasps (gets rejected as non-machinable); listing 50 common chromes one at a time (12 hours invested, $30 in sales); ignoring publisher info on the back; not transcribing the message; pricing from **active** listings instead of sold (active prices are wishful thinking); taping toploaders directly (ruins them); soaking off stamps or "improving" cards (destroys postal history value); cleaning, erasing, or trimming cards (wrecks them — collectors prize honest condition; erasure is detectable under angled light and devalues 50%+; trimmed cards lose 60–80%).

**Sourcing in Alabama:** set EstateSales.NET, EstateSales.org, and gsalr.com alerts for Birmingham/Mobile/Huntsville/Montgomery/Tuscaloosa with keywords "postcards," "ephemera," "collectibles." **Day 1 morning** for the best cards (high-grade RPPCs and organized albums get snapped fast); **Day 3 (half-off or fill-a-bag)** for volume sourcing — this is where you find the $20 shoebox of 500 cards. Look for shoeboxes, cigar boxes, old albums, scrapbooks, family correspondence, "Granddad's military stuff."

**Pass prices to discipline yourself:** estate sale shoebox of 200+ unsorted above $0.30/card avg ($60 for 200) → pass; themed album above $1/card if all identifiable / $0.50 mixed; antique mall single above $2 unless comp is $15+; thrift single above $1; flea market dealer above $0.25–$0.50/card on negotiated bulk.

**Black Belt sourcing angle:** estate sales in older Black Belt towns (Selma, Demopolis, Eufaula, Camden) sometimes yield exceptional pre-1920 Black Americana, courthouse and bridge RPPCs. Pre-1920 anything from Alabama is worth research.

**Reference resources to bookmark and own:**

Free online: **metropolitanpostcardclub.com** (free encyclopedia of publishers, types, eras — single best free reference), **playle.com/realphoto** (RPPC stamp box dating chart), **postcard.org** (International Postcard Association), **Newberry Library Curt Teich Postcard Archives** (searchable 400,000+ Teich production records — can date a card to the year using its production number), **TuckDB.org** (Raphael Tuck database), **cardcow.com** (active retail dealer pricing), **doanecancel.com** (Doane reference), **postcardhistory.net** (articles, repro identification), **britannicauctions.com** blog.

Communities: Reddit r/Postcards, r/Whatnot, r/Flipping, r/Ephemera. Facebook groups: "Vintage Postcards For Sale and Trade," "Real Photo Postcard (RPPC) Collectors," "Halloween Postcard Collectors." eBay Postcard Collectors community board. Delcampe Forums.

Reference books to own: J.L. Mashburn *The Postcard Price Guide* (4th ed.); Mashburn *Fantasy Postcards With Price Guide* (Halloween/Krampus/Santas); Apkarian-Russell *Halloween Postcards Catalog* (the Halloween bible); Susan Brown Nicholson *Encyclopedia of Antique Postcards*; Q. David Bowers *Cordially Yours*; Jeffrey Meikle *Postcard America: Curt Teich and the Imaging of a Nation*.

Clubs and shows: International Federation of Postcard Dealers (ifpd.com), Postcard History Society (postcardhistorysociety.com), Chicago National Postcard Show (March), Western Reserve Postcard Show (Ohio), Atlanta Postcard Club (closest active regional club for you), Southeastern Postcard Club shows.

## Closing — what changes once you internalize this

Most postcard resellers leave money on the table in three predictable ways: they undervalue small-town RPPCs (the single most underpriced category nationally), they ignore postal history details on the back (turning $50 cards into $15 cards by listing the picture but missing the Doane), and they title cards without using the actual search vocabulary collectors type into eBay. AlabamaCollects has a sourcing edge most national sellers don't — local Alabama estate flow into a market that's structurally undersupplied — and the highest-leverage move is **pulling every RPPC and pre-1915 card out of every lot, identifying town/era/publisher/postal-history details fully, and titling them with all 80 characters of keyword-dense terms that real collectors search**. Run the commons through weekly themed Whatnot shows ($1 start, end Sunday afternoon). Lot anything you can't comp at $5+. Photograph both sides. Transcribe the message. Carry a 10× loupe and a copy of Mashburn. The hobby pays workers who treat it like a craft.
